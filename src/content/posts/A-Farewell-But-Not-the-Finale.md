Having watched three years slip away almost imperceptibly, I have only just come to realize how astonishingly fast time has flown. There are still so many things I have long wanted to say, yet never managed to express in reality; therefore, I write this article, hoping to use these slightly untidy but heartfelt words to convey the thoughts that have remained unspoken throughout these years.

During my nearly fifteen years of learning English, I have met many English teachers, each of whom has, to a greater or lesser extent, changed my life and shaped my character. From the warm sunlight in the English office in my primary school, to the only words of encouragement I received from my English teacher before the senior high school entrance examination(according to the ranking-based system then in place, I was regarded as someone who would not be able to enter senior high school, and when no other teacher seemed willing to pay any attention to me except my English teacher),to the broader international perspective opened up by several foreign teachers... Most importantly, at one of the most crucial turning points in my life, I met you.

In the very first month of my high school life, I had already achieved more than I had managed to accomplish throughout all three years of junior high school combined. The first time I stood on the stage before the entire school as the top scorer in a single subject, I felt something I had never felt before. It was not merely joy; more importantly, it was the moment when I began to truly recognize myself and to believe that my abilities were strong enough to carry me to such a height. And then came the story everyone already knows: thirteen times as the top scorer in that subject over the course of three years, a five-time consecutive reign in the early days of my first year, and a six-time consecutive streak toward the end of my senior year. Though I may have long since taken being the top scorer in this subject for granted, looking back, perhaps I was, among all the students who have ever studied here, one of the more outstanding ones?

Perhaps I should not spend this article speaking only about myself; admittedly, it has become rather disorganized. Before I formally entered high school, my English had never been truly systematic, and it was only during these three years that I began to study vocabulary, sentence patterns, and grammar in a structured way. Of course, apart from doing a few practice questions on my own after class, I did not spend much additional time teaching myself these things, so naturally, all credit for that progress should go to you. If I were to sum up everything I learned in high school here, I am afraid that even thirty thousand words would not be enough, so perhaps it is better to measure it in a more direct way: by scores. You once told me that the highest score ever achieved by one of your students was 142, while my personal best at the time was 138.5. As for my goal for the college entrance examination... naturally, it was not merely 140. I do not even know where that confidence came from, but I really did set myself the target of breaking that 142-point record. Perhaps, to some extent, I am a rather pragmatic person; only when I have truly achieved something that can be objectively recognized by others do I feel genuinely satisfied.

Of course, now that I have reached this important point in my journey, I should stop dwelling on these scores, for there are still so many precious memories from my high school years! Some things will never die in my heart; those people and events will be deeply engraved in some corner of my mind, and I suppose I shall never forget them for the rest of my life. Time, please stop for a moment—you are far too cruel! It is hard not to think of a video I once watched, which I would like to place here; if possible, please do take the time to watch it through to the end.

“Why can we never bear to part with those things that once brought warmth and beauty into our lives? The years that keep slipping through our fingers—if only we could hold on to them for just one more second.”

When we sink into the sorrow of departure, we are also reminiscing about that fleeting moment of joy. As we reach out toward those vanishing joys, a sense of loss, happiness, and a faint sadness all rise together; is this not, in a way, a small act of resistance against time’s irretrievability? To savor these tiny fragments of memory from time to time—is that not, too, a rather different kind of feeling? As the video says, “So, whether that happiness was real or rational or stable, please let me linger a little longer over these moments that keep drifting away.” Everything will eventually come to an end, but it is precisely because all things are destined to fade that the brilliance of their blooming, in that very instant, becomes truly beautiful.

After writing so much, everything has become rather fragmented; I have been writing almost entirely as thoughts came to me, which is far more unrestrained than any exam essay could ever be. My high school years have now come to an end, and I was only one student in the long course of your teaching career, so parting was always inevitable. “There is no need to mourn over the farewell cup; those who drink deeply have always carried hearts of their own.” I do not know whether I will ever return to Dalian, whether I will ever again have the chance to step through my alma mater’s gates, or whether we will ever meet again in the future. But in any case, farewell has never been the end; every parting is, after all, a new beginning in life. Thank you for giving me such a beautiful journey, for having appeared in my life, and for shaping the “ego” I am today. This experience, this kindness, I will carry with me for the rest of my life.

So here I lie in our imperfect paradise.
A eulogistic lullaby.

And thank you for everything.

Sincerely,
Zhou Chenyu
